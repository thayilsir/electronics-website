Basic Electronic Components

Resistor

A resistor is a fundamental electronic component that limits the flow of electric current in a circuit. It is passive and does not generate energy.

Resistors work by converting electrical energy into heat, according to Ohm‚Äôs law (V = IR), which defines the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

Applications: voltage division, current limiting, pull-up/down in digital circuits, and biasing of transistors.

Capacitor

A capacitor stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by an insulator (dielectric). It can charge and discharge rapidly.

It works by accumulating charge proportional to the voltage applied (Q = CV), releasing it when needed. Capacitors block DC but pass AC in circuits.

Applications: smoothing power supplies, timing circuits, coupling/decoupling signals, energy storage.

Inductor

An inductor is a passive component that stores energy in a magnetic field created by the current passing through a coiled wire.

It resists changes in current and can filter or smooth signals, store energy in switching circuits, or form resonant LC circuits.

Applications: chokes in power supplies, tuning circuits, transformers, and energy storage in switching regulators.

Electronics Engineering Interview Questions

What is Ohm's Law?

Ohm's Law states that the current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the voltage across the two points and inversely proportional to the resistance: I = V/R.

Relevance: Fundamental for designing circuits, calculating current, voltage drops, and resistor values.

Engineers should know this because nearly every circuit design and analysis depends on it.

Difference between BJT and MOSFET?

BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) is current-controlled, while MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor FET) is voltage-controlled.

Relevance: Choosing the right transistor affects switching speed, power handling, and efficiency.

Engineers need this knowledge for proper selection in amplifiers, switches, and power circuits.

What is a pull-up resistor?

A pull-up resistor connects a digital input to a high logic level to ensure it defaults to HIGH when not driven.

Relevance: Prevents floating inputs which can cause unpredictable behavior.

Essential for engineers designing reliable digital circuits and microcontroller inputs.

Diode

A diode allows current to flow in one direction only, blocking reverse flow. It's a passive component used to control direction of electricity.

Diodes work based on the P-N junction: electrons move from n-type to p-type material when forward biased.

Applications: rectifiers, voltage protection, signal demodulation, LED circuits.

LED (Light Emitting Diode)

An LED emits light when current passes through it in the forward direction. It's energy-efficient and long-lasting compared to traditional bulbs.

It works via electroluminescence: electrons recombine with holes releasing photons of light.

Applications: indicators, displays, lighting, signaling, decorative projects.

Transistor (BJT)

BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) is a current-controlled switch/amplifier with three terminals: collector, base, and emitter.

It works by controlling a large current between collector-emitter using a small base current.

Applications: amplifiers, switching circuits, oscillators, digital logic circuits.

Transistor (MOSFET)

MOSFET is a voltage-controlled transistor with high input impedance, used for switching and amplification.

Gate voltage creates a conductive channel allowing current to flow between drain and source.

Applications: switching power supplies, motor drivers, digital electronics, amplifiers.

IC (Integrated Circuit)

An IC is a miniaturized electronic circuit on a small chip of semiconductor material. It can contain thousands or millions of transistors.

ICs simplify circuit design, reduce size, and improve reliability compared to discrete components.

Applications: microcontrollers, amplifiers, logic gates, timers, memory devices.

Relay

A relay is an electrically operated switch that uses an electromagnet to mechanically operate contacts.

It allows a low-power circuit to control a high-power device safely.

Applications: switching motors, lights, automated systems, isolation of circuits.

Switch

A switch is a simple device to open or close an electrical circuit manually or mechanically.

It works by either connecting or disconnecting the conducting path.

Applications: turning devices on/off, control panels, safety mechanisms.

Potentiometer

A potentiometer is a variable resistor used to adjust voltage or current in a circuit.

It works by sliding a wiper along a resistive track, varying resistance.

Applications: volume controls, brightness adjustment, sensor calibration.

Crystal Oscillator

A crystal oscillator generates precise frequencies using the mechanical resonance of a quartz crystal.

It works via piezoelectric effect: voltage applied causes crystal vibration, generating stable oscillations.

Applications: clocks, microcontroller timing, radio transmitters, communication devices.

Fuse

A fuse is a protective device that breaks a circuit when current exceeds a certain limit, preventing damage or fire.

It works by melting a thin wire or strip inside when excessive current passes.

Applications: household appliances, power circuits, automotive circuits, safety protection.

Transformer

A transformer transfers electrical energy between two circuits using electromagnetic induction.

It works by varying the number of turns in primary and secondary coils to step voltage up or down.

Applications: power distribution, voltage regulation, adapters, electrical isolation.

Photoresistor (LDR)

A photoresistor changes resistance based on light intensity falling on it.

It works because its semiconductor material becomes more conductive when exposed to light.

Applications: automatic lighting, alarms, light meters, solar tracking systems.

Thermistor

A thermistor is a temperature-dependent resistor that changes resistance with temperature.

NTC thermistors decrease resistance with temperature rise, PTC increase.

Applications: temperature measurement, overcurrent protection, temperature compensation circuits.

Hall Effect Sensor

A Hall sensor detects magnetic fields and converts them into voltage or current signals.

It works by the Hall effect: voltage develops across a conductor when placed in a magnetic field perpendicular to current.

Applications: brushless motors, position sensing, speed detection, proximity sensors.

Piezo Buzzer

A piezo buzzer converts electrical signals into sound using piezoelectric materials.

It works by applying AC voltage to piezo material, making it vibrate and produce audible sound.

Applications: alarms, timers, notification signals, simple music generators.

Capacitive Touch Sensor

A capacitive sensor detects touch by measuring changes in capacitance caused by a human finger.

It works without mechanical movement, making devices more durable and responsive.

Applications: touchscreens, buttons, lamps, smart devices.

Microphone

A microphone converts sound waves into electrical signals.

It works using dynamic (coil+magnet) or condenser (capacitor+diaphragm) methods to convert vibrations to voltage changes.

Applications: audio recording, communication, voice recognition, sound detection circuits.

What is the difference between BJT and MOSFET?

Answer: BJT is current-controlled, while MOSFET is voltage-controlled. BJT uses base current to control collector current; MOSFET uses gate voltage to control drain-source current.

Relevance: Understanding switching and amplification characteristics is crucial for circuit design.

Why engineers should know: Helps select appropriate transistor type for amplifiers, switches, and digital circuits.

Explain the function of a capacitor in a DC circuit.

Answer: In DC, a capacitor initially charges, then blocks steady current while storing energy in the electric field.

Relevance: Capacitors are used for filtering, energy storage, and timing circuits.

Why engineers should know: Enables design of power supplies, timing circuits, and noise suppression circuits effectively.

What is the purpose of a pull-up/pull-down resistor?

Answer: Pull-up/pull-down resistors set a default logic level on a pin when no input is present.

Relevance: Prevents floating pins that may cause unpredictable behavior.

Why engineers should know: Critical for reliable digital circuit design and microcontroller input stability.

Explain the difference between AC and DC power.

Answer: DC flows in one direction; AC alternates direction periodically.

Relevance: AC is used for power distribution; DC for electronics and batteries.

Why engineers should know: Helps in selecting power sources, designing converters, and understanding energy transmission.

Why engineers should know: Essential for designing energy-efficient electrical systems and minimizing losses.
